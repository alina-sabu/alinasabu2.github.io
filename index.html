Abstract


This research project aims to explore the attitudes and preferences of different generations of immigrant communities towards localisation strategies for maintaining and promoting their heritage language in Ireland. To exemplify the concept of localisation, two news websites were developed with identical content but different languages and positioning of content -one in English and the other in Malayalam. The study involves conducting two surveys, targeting both Malayali immigrants and immigrants of other nationalities residing in Ireland.

Participants will be asked about their age, ethnicity, immigrant generation, proficiency level in their mother tongue, typical language usage situations, and frequency of use. They will also be asked to compare the two news websites and provide feedback on the effectiveness of localisation strategies, as well as the challenges involved in implementing them.

The project's findings will enhance the understanding of the efficiency of localisation strategies in promoting and sustaining heritage languages in immigrant communities in Ireland. It will offer insights into how different generations of immigrants view localisation strategies, as well as the challenges that must be addressed to implement them. Overall, this research project intends to support the preservation and promotion of heritage languages, not only in Ireland but also worldwide.

      Language is a vital part of human identity and culture, and the maintenance and promotion of heritage languages is an important aspect of immigrant communities. Ireland has experienced a significant increase in its immigrant population in recent years, with over 700,000 people [1]. living in the country who were born outside of Ireland. The attitudes and preferences of immigrant communities towards language maintenance and promotion have important implications for both the communities themselves and the wider Irish society.       Heritage Language:

      According to Kelleher [3], the term heritage language is used to identify languages other than the dominant language (or languages) in a particular social context. In relation to Ireland, English is the primary language used in government, education, and public communication, therefore any language other than English is considered a "heritage language" for speakers of that language. Many times, it is used interchangeably with the term ‘minority language’ or ‘home language’. As explained by Kelleher, Joshua Fishman (2001) identifies three types of Heritage language:

      1. Immigrant heritage languages are any of the languages spoken by immigrants arriving in the country after it became an independent one. For instance, Spanish would be the heritage language of a Spanish immigrant in Ireland.

      2. Indigenous heritage languages are the languages of those native to the country. In Ireland, Irish is the indigenous heritage language.

      3. Colonial heritage language is the language of the colonists in the country. Though this is not quite applicable in Ireland, technically, the colonial heritage language here would be English, the dominant language of the country.


        Localisation:

        Localization is the practice of modifying a product, service, or content to suit a particular market or location, which may involve altering elements such as language, currency, or time zones to cater to the specific needs of a particular culture or population. Localization is crucial for companies that seek to expand their reach and enter new markets. Unlike translation, localization encompasses more than just language and considers cultural and regional disparities. Effective localization is characterized by a product or service that appears to be developed within the local culture[4].

        Localization strategy is a plan to modify a product or service to meet the needs of a particular language, culture, or population in a target market. A localization strategy can offer several advantages to companies, including minimizing communication errors, avoiding the use of culturally inappropriate language, building brand trust and loyalty, gaining a competitive advantage, appearing user-friendly to local customers, and demonstrating commitment to a particular market [5]. These benefits are particularly important for companies seeking to

        expand their business globally, as they can help ensure successful market penetration and growth. However, it should be noted that localization strategies are not limited to commercial uses and can also be employed by non-profit organizations, government agencies, and other entities seeking to reach diverse audiences. The goal of a non-commercial localization strategy is to create a localized experience that is culturally sensitive and relevant to the target audience, and to promote understanding and engagement with the community. A non-commercial localization involves the same strategies such as translation, cultural adaptation, and customization of communication materials to meet the needs and preferences of the target audience [6].

        Immigrant generations:

        Immigrant generation is a central sociological factor that affects the assimilation of immigrants and their descendants. It categorizes individuals based on birthplace, birthplace of parents, and length of time in the country. Traditionally, the three main categories are 1st generation (foreign born), 2nd generation (native born children of foreign born or mixed parents), and 3rd and higher generations (native born children of native-born parents) [7].

        Rumbaut (2004) examines issues related to defining and identifying "first" and "second" generations of immigrants in the United States. He suggested that the definition of immigrant generations can be clearer and avoid confusion by distinguishing generational cohorts (1.0, 1.25, 1.5, 1,75, 2.0, 2.25). These cohorts are defined based on the age and life stage at migration for foreign-born individuals and the parental nativity for U.S.-born individuals. He analyses aspects of acculturations such as differences in education, occupation, language, and other acculturation aspects to support his theory that the context of social development and generational groups can affect the way people adapt and achieve social mobility [8].


        Theories

        The theories of Immigration integration, as well as Language Maintenance and Shift Theory will be examined in more detail in the following section.

        Immigrant Integration:

        In recent times, there are an increasing number of metrics for measuring immigrant integration into host societies in North America and Europe. Historically, governments focused on assimilation but now there is more emphasis on cooperation between immigrants and host societies to create a more supportive community [9]. Three theories of immigration integration are explored here:

        • Assimilation theory [10], which has been the dominant perspective for much of the twentieth century, proposes that diverse ethnic groups gradually become integrated into a common culture, with their original cultural patterns gradually disappearing. The classical assimilation concept is often criticized for portraying the incorporation of immigrants into the host society as a process of complete surrender of their original culture in favour of the dominant culture. This perspective implies that assimilation should be a two-way process, where the host society also adapts and becomes more inclusive of diverse cultural backgrounds [11].

        • A second theory is that of Multiculturalism [10], which, in contrast rejects the simple integration process proposed by assimilation theory, viewing society as a collection of different ethnic and racial minority groups and a dominant majority group, with immigrants actively shaping their own identities. The multicultural perspective views members of ethnic minority groups as active and integral parts of the larger society, rather than simply being seen as foreigners or outsiders. It recognizes the value and importance of cultural diversity and encourages the maintenance of distinct cultural identities while also promoting a sense of unity and mutual respect within the larger society.

        • The structuralist approach [10] to cultural integration does not focus on the processes of assimilation or integration, but rather on how socio-economic differences in socioeconomic opportunities affect the social integration of ethnic minority groups. Structural constraints, such as unequal access to wealth, jobs, education, power, and privilege, can hinder the ability of immigrants and ethnic minorities to socially integrate, resulting in persistent ethnic disparities in levels of income, education, and occupational achievement. The advantages of integration vary significantly based on the level of society that accepts the incoming immigrants. Unlike assimilation theory and multiculturalism, structuralism highlights the inherent conflicts in the social hierarchy between dominant and minority groups, questioning the possibility of cultural and socio-economic integration of immigrants.

          After examining the various theories of cultural integration, attention can now be directed towards the measurement of integration. Although there is no universally applicable method, indicators such as rates of literacy, employment, naturalization, educational attainment, economic status, political participation, and access to services are commonly employed. However, this approach has limitations in terms of capturing meaningful nuances in outcomes among specific groups and measuring the role of societies around them. Recent indicators have broadened to include efforts by governments and host communities to provide access to employment, education, welfare services, and healthcare, as well as policies focused on antidiscrimination, family reunification, permanent residence, and political participation. The success of individual immigrants can also offer insight into the social, economic, and political integration of immigrants in host societies. Regardless of the persistent challenge in measuring immigrant integration, new indicators like mentioned are being employed and tested [9].

          The Migration Policy Institute [12] released a brief which provides policymakers with an overview of cultural competency and its potential application in developing effective policies to promote immigrant integration. Cultural competencies refer to the ability to understand and respect cultural diversity by recognizing and valuing differences in beliefs and values between cultures. Multiple policy areas, such as education, healthcare, social services, and public safety are examined where cultural competency can be applied to achieve better outcomes for immigrant families and their communities. The brief also discusses strategies for cultivating a culturally competent workforce, which is critical to promoting immigrant integration and reducing disparities in accessing services. The paper emphasizes the importance of a culturally competent approach to immigration policy in promoting an inclusive and equitable society. The significance of cultural competency in the development of comprehensive immigration reform is emphasised, suggesting that a culturally competent approach to immigration policy can foster a more inclusive and equitable society.

          Maintenance and Shift Theory:

          The LMLS (Language Maintenance and Shift) framework is a model that seeks to explain the complex factors that contribute to the maintenance or shift of minority languages among immigrant communities. The concept of language maintenance involves protecting and promoting the use of a native language within communities of linguistic minorities. Within such communities, bilingualism can be sustained through the use of the societal language for formal purposes while using the heritage language for social interactions. Unfortunately, minority populations are frequently coerced into assimilating into the mainstream society, or they willingly abandon their language, leading to a shift in language usage [13].

          As summarised by Dagamesh [14], the contributions of three prominent scholars in the field of sociolinguistics have enhanced our understanding of language maintenance and language shift. These scholars are Fishman (1966), Weinreich (1979), and Kloss (1971):

        • Fishman's (1966) model focuses on the domains of language use, psychological, social, and cultural factors affecting the use of minority languages, and attitudes towards these languages. Fishman (1989) proposed that language shift in minority societies mostly occurs within three generations, with the first generation being largely monolingual in their heritage language, the second generation being bilingual in the heritage and majority language, and the third generation being mainly monolingual in the majority language. Fishman (1989) also argued that cultural and linguistic change is inevitable and anticipated.

        • Kloss (1966) identified ambiguous factors that can either encourage or discourage language maintenance which can be grouped and combined to create broader categories of analysis. These factors include the educational level of immigrants, numerical strength of the minority group, cultural and linguistic similarity or dissimilarity to the majority group, and language policy of the majority group.

        • Weinreich (1979) proposed that external factors like cultural or ethnic groups, religion, race, sex, age, geographic areas, indigenousness, social status, occupation, and rural vs. urban population can impact the linguistic features of a minority group. He also explained the relationship between language loyalty and shift, defining language loyalty as a principle that motivates people to resist changes in language functions, structure or vocabulary.

        By applying the LMLS framework to the research question of this study, it is possible to explore how llinguistic, societal, and individual factors interact to influence language maintenance and shift across different generations of immigrant communities. The role of identity and cultural attitudes towards language maintenance are also considered within this theoretical framework.

        2.1b Studies

        In order to understand the existing research on the language maintenance and transmission of heritage languages across immigrant generations, three studies that represent different ethnicities and host countries were reviewed: Linguistic Life Expectancies: Immigrant Language Retention in Southern California (Rumbaut et al, 2006), Maintaining the heritage language and identity: the case of the new diaspora Telugus in London (Yadla, 2016) and New

        Speakers in the Irish Context: Heritage Language Maintenance Among Multilingual Migrants in Dublin, Ireland (Diskin, 2020).

        Rumbaut et al. [15] conducted a research study that explores the language retention patterns of immigrants in Southern California. The study aims to examine the linguistic life expectancy of immigrants and to identify the factors that influence language maintenance and shift. The study also examines whether the Spanish language, unlike other immigrant languages in the US, will resist English language dominance over generations. The study draws data from two sources: the Immigration and Intergenerational Mobility in Metropolitan Los Angeles (IIMMLA) survey and the third wave of the Children of Immigrants Longitudinal Study (CILS) in San Diego. These sources were merged to create a sample size of 5,703, with respondents evenly divided by sex and of the same approximate age and national origin. The focus was on language assimilation patterns of the 1.5 generation (immigrants who arrived in the US as children) and the second generation (US-born children of immigrant parents). The two surveys used identical measures of English and non-English language proficiency and preference, and of other relevant variables. The merging of the two data sets provides larger sample sizes for significant subgroups and greater precision and reliability for estimating linguistic life expectancies by group and generation. The study concluded that the linguistic life expectancy of immigrants is determined by various factors such as age at migration, duration of stay in the United States, and ethnic concentration. It was found that Spanish-speaking groups have higher generational survival curves compared to Asians and white Europeans in Southern California, with a higher proportion of Spanish-speaking individuals in each generational point from 1.5 to 2.5. However, in the third generation and beyond, the survival curves begin to converge, with only 5 percent of Mexicans speaking fluent Spanish by the fourth generation. Overall, the study concludes that language maintenance is a complex process that is influenced by a range of social and cultural factors.

        Yadla’s study [16] explores the language maintenance and shift among Telugu-speaking immigrants in London. The research was conducted through a mixed-method approach, which included a survey, face-to-face interactions, family interviews, focus groups, and observational fieldwork. The 109 study participants consisted of both first and second-generation Telugu-speaking immigrants, who were divided into two waves based on the time of their arrival in the UK. The analysis of language proficiency showed that the first-generation immigrants were proficient in both Telugu and English, as they acquired English language skills before migrating to the UK and learned Telugu during their primary education in India. The study also found that language loyalty differed with respect to the time of arrival and the conditions of living. The first-generation immigrants showed continued maintenance of Telugu, while the second generation showed evidence of language shift towards English, which was more dominant in their daily lives. The study also explored the factors contributing to language maintenance and shift among Telugu-speaking immigrants. It found that the maintenance of a language depends on its usage, specifically, speaking it on a regular basis. The wave 1 study participants' exposure to Telugu was limited, and their contact with India was based on occasional visits, which did not assist in the maintenance of the heritage language. Moreover, confirming the results of the previously reviewed study, it was found that the second-generation participants did not seem to have a favourable attitude towards learning to read and write Telugu as opposed to speaking it. In conclusion, the study suggests that language shift among Telugu-speaking immigrants in London is evident, as most second-generation immigrants use English even at home. The study

        also highlights the importance of community strength and the value placed on language for ethnic maintenance, which can influence the maintenance or shift of a language among immigrant communities.

        Diskin’s study [17] explores the attitudes and strategies towards heritage language (HL) maintenance and transmission among Polish and Chinese migrants in Dublin, Ireland. The study aimed to identify recurring themes relating to motivations for HL maintenance and identity, as well as to investigate the use of print and digital media for maintaining and transmitting the heritage language. The study used a qualitative approach, based on semi-structured interviews with 45 migrants (23 Polish and 22 Chinese) living in Dublin. The interviews were conducted in the participants' heritage language and were audio-recorded and transcribed. The transcripts were then analysed using a content analysis approach to identify recurring themes and patterns. The study found that maintaining the heritage language was of paramount importance for all generations of migrants in both communities. Many parents sent their children to weekend schools to learn their heritage language and culture. Polish weekend schools were successful in teaching the Polish language and culture to children, while the Chinese community lacked similar resources. It was also found that maintaining literacy in the heritage language was important for both communities, and they used various strategies to achieve this, including using print and digital media. The participants reported using their heritage language on a daily basis and placing strong emphasis on maintaining literacy through reading, writing and social media communication. The attitudes and motivations for engaging in HL maintenance strategies were summarised into four main themes: mobility, family ties/heritage, education and economic factors. The Chinese participants viewed Mandarin as a global language important for participating in the international job market, while the Poles viewed Polish as important for maintaining heritage, culture and history, and the nurturing of familial ties. Overall, the study highlights the importance of language in constructing and maintaining a migrant identity and the use of language as a mobile resource. The findings suggest that the maintenance of heritage languages will become more diverse and complex in the future. The study provides insight into the experiences and opportunities of HL maintenance among two different migrant communities in Dublin.

        These three reviewed studies shed light on the complex and dynamic nature of language maintenance and shift among immigrant communities, with a particular focus on the preservation and transmission of heritage languages across generations.


        Since an aspect of focus of this research project is on the perspective of localisation Malayali's in Ireland have, the following review will specifically address the assimilation of immigrants.

        According to the Central Statistics Office [18], out of the total population of Ireland, 12.9% or approximately 645,500 people are non-Irish nationals. The top three non-Irish nationalities residing in Ireland are Polish, UK, and Lithuanian, followed by several other nationalities including Indian and Pakistani. The bulk of the community is in health-care, IT, engineering and senior management positions [19]. An article by Pollack in 2019 [20] discusses the experiences of Indian nurses who migrated to Ireland from the southern Indian state of Kerala, seeking better job prospects and working conditions. The article cites statistics from the 2016 census, which show that almost 21,000 Indian-born nationals were living in Ireland, with half speaking Malayalam, the principal language of Kerala. The Nursing and Midwifery Board

        confirms that in 2018, 9% of all registered nurses were Indian nationals, with the number of successful visa applications from India rising sharply from 79 in 2014 to 753 in 2017. The author’s interviews with Indian nurses in Ireland revealed that emigrating nurses were seeking job security and better pay to provide for their families, with many sending money back home to support their parents. The article shows that, while emigration offers new opportunities and a better quality of life, it comes at a significant cost to the social and emotional fabric of families and communities left behind.

        As well this, It is a common challenge for immigrant parents to raise their children in a cultural and linguistic environment that differs from their ethnicity. This is due to the difficulty in instilling cultural and linguistic pride of their origin into their children. Yadla’s study on Telugu immigrants found that second-generation participants had little exposure to the Telugu language and script, and even the first generation rated their Telugu skills lower than English. The reason for this is that gradual assimilation into the host society can result in the natural erosion of an individual's original cultural practices and skills [16]. A vocabulary test was also conducted to evaluate the language skills of second-generation Telugu immigrants in the UK. The participants were shown pictures of common items found in Telugu households and were asked to recall the corresponding Telugu names. The results showed that items typical of Telugu cuisine and fruits specific to the Telugu region were easier to recall, while familiarity with celebrated Telugu dishes depended on household food habits. Pan-Indian popular items were more familiar, and only those born in India could identify aspects beyond food. Overall, the study reveals the challenges faced by immigrant parents in preserving their cultural and linguistic heritage in a different environment.

        It is also possible that socio-cultural assimilation can slow down if excessive involvement of immigrants with their ethnic associations and subsequent ethnic bonding take place. One such example is a study by Mukherjee and Pattnaik [21] that analysed the assimilation dynamics of a small Bengali immigrant community in Overland Park, Kansas City, USA . The community formed a closely-knit group and sustained their bonding social capital through activities of ethnic associations, consumption of ethnic amenities, and weekend parties. The study found that this ethnic bonding, combined with the low ethnic/racial diversity in the city, contributed to slow and limited assimilation of Bengalis into American society. The study shows that assimilation is segmental, and ethnic associations not only provide a comfort zone to immigrants in a culturally and racially different country but also play a prominent role in preserving the ethnic identity of their members.


        This section concerns the role played by ethnic media within immigrant communities:

        The emergence of the ethnic media landscape in Ireland 2007, led to divisions as different outlets struggle to convert the influx of new people into an influx of new readers, listeners, and viewers. While some argue that English is the best way to communicate with Ireland's new residents, others argue the merits of communicating with them through their own languages. The English-language model gained momentum when the Nigerian journalists behind 'Metro Eireann' launched a new magazine in English in 2000. In contrast, the foreign language model had also grown. In 2006 and 2007, in tribute to Polish immigrants, a Polish supplement called the Polski Herald was published once a week. The supplement was a response to the growing number of Polish-speaking immigrants arriving in Ireland after the EU accession of ten new

        members on May 1, 2004. Its aim was to support the increasing number of Polish nationals living in Ireland by providing local community information and news from their home country. There were also at least 20 other Irish foreign language titles on the market that year such as Turkish, French, Polish, Lithuanian, and Russian [22]. The role of advertising was also seen changing at the time. As the ethnic groups became more rooted in Irish society, advertisers were beginning to show interest in targeting specific ethnic groups [23].

        Ethnic media play a vital role in assisting immigrants in connecting with news and events from their home country, while also providing orientation to their new community and country [24]. This is known as the connective and orientation function. The advancement of communication technologies has made it easier and faster for immigrants to access ethnic media. An effective balance of both types of stories contributes to a dual frame of reference for immigrants, enabling them to understand the norms of their home country and their host country, and utilize both sets of cultural rules. Ethnic media can serve as valuable resources for immigrants by fulfilling both connective and orientation functions. However, some ethnic media sources may only focus on home country news, which can discourage immigrants from looking outward to their new community. To help foster a sense of belonging, ethnic media should also provide stories about the local community. An example was provided of a study of Chinese, Korean, and Spanish-language ethnic newspapers in Los Angeles found that the ratio of home country to local community news has consequences for immigrants’ sense of belonging to their new community. Ethnic newspapers that provided both home country and local news had higher levels of community engagement. Despite their usefulness, not all ethnic media outlets provide the perfect balance between connective and orientation-related content. Some may focus mostly on the bare necessities of temporary settlement, with most content related to ways to remain fully connected to the home country. Immigrants with limited English language proficiency may also face literacy barriers, which makes ethnic radio broadcasts an important alternative for staying informed about news and events. Despite the absence of formal studies regarding ethnic media use among the Irish Malayali community, three prominent Malayali-owned news sites have emerged: https://irishmalayali.ie/ , https://www.rosemalayalam.com/ , and https://irishvanitha.com/ . These sites offer news stories from Kerala, as well as stories about Irish and UK Malayalis, and other relevant topics that may be of interest to their readers. Advertisements from both Malayali and Irish businesses can also be found on these sites. While there is no data available to indicate readership figures, the existence of multiple news sites suggests that there is enough demand among the Irish Malayali community for such media.

The concept of ethnic media and its examples in Ireland were also discussed.

  3. Design


    3a Architecture of site

    The code for the systems describes a simple client-side web application architecture that consists of two websites built using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The aim of the sites was to provide a simple contrast between the original (English) and Malayalam versions of the sites to survey respondents therefore the structural architecture was kept minimal. It was important that the sites followed a prototypical design and the only obvious difference between the two sites were to be the language and presentation of content. Both sites followed a common layout and structure, with a header, main content section, and footer. The header includes a navigation bar with access to different sections of the site. This navigation bar is not functional and intended only to mimic other sites to invoke a feeling of familiarity and fluency to users. Sites with high level of fluency and familiarity results in users spending less time navigating so they can instead focus on the purpose of their visit [25]. The JavaScript code enables the implementation of a responsive menu button that toggles the display of the navigation bar on smaller screens. The main content section contains news articles, and media files such as images and videos. The only linked item on this site is the word “English” or “മലയാളം”

    (Malayalam) that appears at the top right of the navigation bar which allows users to easily switch between both versions of the site. Overall, the system is designed for simple display, navigation, and functionality to maintain focus on the localisation strategies that are used.


    3b Design Decision

    The navigation and footer bars are identical in both versions of the site even regarding language – both are in English. The reason for the English headings in the Malayalam version was due to the many examples witnessed in Malayalam News websites where this was shown. English is used widely in Indian printed media [26] so it is not unusual to see English headings, sentences or even articles on Malayalam News Sites. Another reason English words were used freely throughout the Malayalam site is that the research that was being conducted was aimed at Non-Residents of India (NRIs) in Ireland. It is only natural that more English language terms and features are adopted by immigrants in a new country they have lived in for some time [27]. Therefore, keeping abbreviations such as “H.S.E” and Irish words such as “Sinn Féin” in its original form, the site is better-localised to suit the Irish-Malayali population as opposed to a Malayali population in Kerala for example.

    The layout and presentation of the sites was heavily influenced by existing examples of Irish and Malayalam News sites to ensure localisation efforts were accurate. The English site was based on sites such as www.rte.ie and www.independent.ie where white space and clean User Interface was emphasised. In contrast to this, Malayalam news sites such as https://www.rosemalayalam.com/ and www.asianetnews.com portray busier interfaces with less white space and a higher emphasis on visual media particularly in the form of video where many News Channels also promote their Live TV on their News sites. This inspired the Malayalam version of the site to include more videos as opposed to its English counterpart. Though a Live TV duplicate could not be sourced as the main video for the Malayalam site, a

    video explaining a news headline of the day was inserted instead. Videos were also added at the bottom of the site. Apart from the difference in media formats, the content on both the sites is identical. To better convey the dynamic and active nature of a Malayalam News site, a marquee text element was incorporated under the header and contributed to the overall sense of movement commonly found in Malayalam websites.


    3c Independent System

    The sites, as mentioned, were developed from scratch using HTML, CSS and JavaScript which are the core technologies for building static websites. Visual Studio Code was used as the Integrated Development Environment (IDE) for coding and editing the website's files, while Github was used as the hosting platform for deploying the website.     1. Methods


    1. a Methodological Framework

      Fishman's (1965) theory of domains, functions, and role-relationships for language use was a framework used in this survey. This theory categorizes language use as either informal or formal, depending on the context. Language use can be grouped loosely into these three domains [28].

      • Intimate: This includes language use in the home, with family and close friends.

      • Social: This includes language use in social settings, such as with acquaintances or in informal social situations.

      • Public: This includes language use in formal settings, such as in the workplace, school, or government.

        According to Fishman, using a language in a particular domain contributes to the language's vitality as well as its speaker’s ability to maintain and transmit it to future generations, hence, the more domains in which a language is used, the more vital it is.

        To understand their language use, survey participants were asked about the frequency of their interactions with different people and in different settings.


        3.1b Design:

        This study will adopt a mixed-methods approach, combining both quantitative and qualitative data collection methods. A mixed method approach typically uses a combination or integration

        of quantitative and qualitative methods [29]. Large amounts of data that can be numerically quantified can be analysed using a quantitative method. Whereas quantitative data answers questions pertaining to numbers, and frequency, qualitative data allows for a more open-ended analysis of the questions through the analysis of the participant surveys.

        In this case, questions with regards to respondents’ demographics such as age, immigrant generation and race will be classed as quantitative. Self-reported language skills and language use in various domains of interaction will be a mix of qualitative and quantitative as there are options as well as long-text fields where respondents can input information. Attitudes towards localisation strategies in maintaining and promoting their heritage language will be mostly qualitative as respondents are asked to explain their thinking through the long-text fields.

        3.1c Participants:

        All participants had to be immigrants and over 18. All participation was voluntary. Participation in the first survey was limited to the Irish Malayali community of any generation. The participants were recruited through snowball sampling, with the initial participants being selected through a Malayali community near the researcher’s area. The participants for the second survey were limited to Irish immigrants. These participants were recruited through University College Cork’s survey mailing list. All participants were presented with a participant information sheet before commencing the survey which informed them about the purpose and procedure of the research. These information sheets had to be checked to continue with the task.

        3.1d Data Collection:

        The survey questionnaires were designed to collect data on the demographics of the respondents i.e., their age range, ethnicities, and immigrant generation, their usage of heritage language and lastly, their attitudes and preferences towards localisation strategies in maintaining and promoting their heritage language. The questionnaires were administered online using Google Forms and included both closed-ended and open-ended questions to enable the collection of both quantitative and qualitative data and allow for a variety of insights. The data is kept strictly confidential and stored in the University’s Google Drive for a maximum of 2 years before being destroyed.

        3.1e Data Analysis:

        The data received from the surveys were analysed using the recommended optimal methods from [29]. The quantitative data collected through the survey after going through data validation, will be analysed using descriptive statistics which summarizes or describes the characteristics of a data set. Examples of descriptive statistics include frequency distributions and measures of central tendency. The qualitative data collected were analysed using thematic analysis, which involves familiarising oneself with the data as well as identifying and analysing patterns in the data. The data was then used to create graphs and charts using Goggle Forms and Sheets. Qualitative data were presented in comprehensive tables created on MS Word.

        3.1f Validity and Reliability:

        A small group of test participants were pilot tested with the survey questionnaires before full implementation to ensure full reliability and validity of the study. The responses of these respondents were observed, and any feedback received on the difficulties and understandings

        of the survey were taken into consideration to refine the survey questions to ensure total comprehension. The consistency of the data collection process across all participants were ensured by providing a standardized survey to all participants, avoiding any possibilities of bias in the participant selection process, and collecting and analysing data using statistical techniques to reduce potential researcher bias in the interpretation of results. This pilot test was valuable to the researcher to identify mistakes or unintentional confusion. One such example of this was the last question, which was initially posed as an open-ended question: “In your opinion, what are some of the challenges associated with implementing effective localization strategies for preserving minority languages in Ireland?” The previous two questions also had open-ended aspects so by the last question, students were not interested in thinking of or answering a long question. This led to the formation of the question as multiple choice, where respondents could just tick what they thought was correct and if desired, could add any other options into the ‘other’ box. It is certain, that had that potential edit been missed, the last question would have had many blank answers which would have been a substantial flaw in the study.

        3.1g Bias:

        The study acknowledges potential sources of bias that may have influenced the design, execution, and interpretation of results. The researcher's personal experiences and perspectives as a member of the 1.5 generation cohort of Malayali origin may have impacted the selection of research questions and data interpretation. Additionally, the survey was distributed through a local Malayali community, which could have introduced selection bias due to the researcher's familiarity with some members. Although efforts were made to mitigate these biases, such as using the snowball method [30] and not using direct family or friends as participants, the possibility of bias remains. The study's limitations should be considered when interpreting the results, and future research should explore alternative sampling strategies and measures to reduce bias.

        3.1h Alternative Methods:

        Other methods of data collection such as in-depth interviews or observations were considered in this study however it was ultimately decided that surveys were the most appropriate method given the target population and time constraints. Surveys enable researchers to gather greater amounts of data from larger populations therefore achieving greater statistical power [31].

    In order to implement the research project, a combination of programming languages and tools were used. Apart from using the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to build the site, Visual Studio Code was used as the Integrated Development Environment (IDE) and GitHub to deploy the website.     4a Data Structures

    Data structures for all three languages were used. As mentioned, HTML describes the structure and content of the website. The main data structures in HTML is tags. Tags give web browsers information about a web page, such as where to display images and the structure of the text [32]. Examples of tags include <header>, <nav>, <section>, <div>, <p>, <img> and <a> which organizes content into different sections and define various elements such as headings, paragraph, images, and links. CSS is closely linked to HTML as this is what is used to style the HTML elements. CSS selectors are used to target HTML elements. Examples include: CSS id selector, CSS element selector and CSS class selectors [33]. In the code for the websites, classes are used to separate the main contents sections from each other. For example: container-top-left and main-container-right. These are applied to HTML elements which gives them desired styles such as font colors, styles and layout properties. Media queries were also utilized in the CSS code of the websites which allows them to be responsive and flexible to various devices and screen sizes. JavaScript was used to enable interactive elements to websites. In this case, it was used to make a responsive hamburger menu for the navigation menu. The code was written to include two event listeners that could toggle the display of the hamburger and times buttons as well as remove or add a class from the navigation bar that displayed or hid the navigation links. Additionally, it contained conditional statements that checked the hamburger button's class name to determine whether or not it should be displayed.

It was challenging to build a visually aesthetic site in all screen sizes and devices styles and constant adjustments to layout properties of elements were made as an attempt to resolve this. Experiments with flexbox layouts, percentage-based widths and other approaches were conducted. Several videos, tutorials and articles on media queries and CSS were researched and though time-consuming successful implementation of a responsive design using CSS and media queries was achieved. There were many unknown criteria for files uploaded to the site that were only comprehended and corrected after several instances of trial and error.   2. Evaluation:

    5a Testing

    Both the English and Malayalam sites were tested on the Google client-side performance tester, Google Page Speed Insights. Google’s Page Speed Insights service is one that analyses a website's content and gives recommendations to accelerate page load times. A fast-loading webpage can decrease the likelihood of users leaving before the page is fully loaded. If a user lands on a website but leaves after viewing only one page, it is referred to as a bounce. A high bounce rate suggests that users are unsatisfied with the website's speed, functionality, or performance. Faster loading times can enhance user experience, increase engagement, and improve the chances of a user acting on the website, such as making a purchase, which is known as a conversion. Therefore, reducing page load times can lower bounce rates and increase conversion rates [36].

    You can view The Page Speed Insights report for both the English and Malayalam websites in Appendix C.

    The Page Speed Insights tool report provides insights and recommendations for improving the websites’ performance and user experience on both desktop and mobile devices. Due to insufficient real-world speed data on the sites, it was not possible to view the ‘real users experience’ feature. However, the report, which is divided into numerous sections, includes a detailed summary of the website's performance score (out of 100), the site load time, as well as the size of the website's resources. It also includes a breakdown of the website's performance metrics, such as First Contentful Paint, Speed Index, Time to Interactive, and others. Interestingly, both websites had quite different results using this tool. The mobile version of the English website had lower scores than the Malayalam versions of my sites with scores for Performance, Accessibility, Best Practises and Search Engine Optimisation (SEO) judged 51, 66, 92 and 67 respectively. This contrasts with the scores of 90, 60, 83, and 67 for my Malayalam site in the same order of categories. For desktop versions of the sites, the scores were within the same range with the average scores for the categories in that order were 70, 63, 87 and 60. The main aspect of improvement suggested was in First and Largest Contentful Paint (FCP and LCP) times which also massively increased the Total Blocking Time. The report includes definitions of concepts and abbreviations as well as recommendations for improving the website's overall score, such as optimizing images, leveraging browser caching, reducing the number of requests, and minimizing JavaScript and CSS resources. Overall, the report provides a detailed analysis of the website's performance and offers actionable insights and suggestions for improving its user experience and search engine ranking.

      Analysis of results:

      The survey results reveal several interesting findings regarding the attitudes and preferences of the Malayali immigrant community towards localization strategies.

      • Firstly, it was observed that despite over 66% of the participants being from 1.5 and second-generation cohorts, the majority considered themselves fluent in Malayalam, which seems to contradict findings from studies reviewed in this report such as Rumbaut et al [15] and Yadla [16], that fluency capabilities decrease across generational cohorts. This could suggest that the Malayali community in Ireland has been successful in maintaining their heritage language. However, it could also be because of Kloss’ (1966) [14] ambiguous factors that encourage language maintenance such as numeric strength in minority group. This can be assumed when the survey found that participants did not restrict the use of Malayalam to their family but also used it with friends and at home. This may explain their reported fluency in the language, as exposure and frequency of use are key factors in maintaining language proficiency.

      • Secondly, despite the participants' claimed fluency, eight out of 12 respondents preferred the English version of the website, while only four preferred the Malayalam

        version. This suggests that while participants may be fluent in spoken Malayalam, they may have difficulty reading and comprehending written Malayalam, which could impact their interest in localization strategies. Furthermore, many respondents did not see a need for localization, possibly due to difficulties in reading or comprehending the Malayalam text. This lack of interest in localization could be attributed to the younger generation's proficiency in spoken Malayalam but limited proficiency in written Malayalam. This can also be seen when some respondents over the age of 40 expressed a desire for localization in education and media outlets particularly for future generations, many did not consider it practical, potentially for the same reasons – the younger generations low proficiency in reading Malayalam.

      • Overall, the survey results provide valuable insights into the attitudes and preferences of the Malayali immigrant community towards localization strategies and suggest that fluency may not always translate to proficiency in written language, which should be considered in future research.

  3. Conclusions

In conclusion, this research project has provided valuable insights into the attitudes and preferences of different generations of immigrant communities towards localisation strategies in maintaining and promoting their heritage language in Ireland. The findings from both the Malayalam and immigrant surveys suggest that there is a strong interest in preserving and promoting minority languages in Ireland, but there are also concerns about the practicality of implementing localisation strategies.

The project has also highlighted that the attitudes and preferences towards localisation strategies vary across different generations of immigrant communities. While there is a clear interest in maintaining and promoting heritage languages among first-generation immigrants, younger generations may be less interested in localisation efforts due to their fluency in English and lack of exposure to their heritage language in written form.

Future developments in this area could include further research on Heritage Language maintenance across generations in Ireland to better understand the pathways of language maintenance and the impact of globalisation on minority languages. Additionally, exploring the potential of technology in promoting the use of minority languages could be a promising avenue for future research. Overall, this project has contributed to our understanding of the complexities surrounding language maintenance and the role of localisation strategies in supporting the preservation and promotion of heritage languages in Ireland.
